9.1  Light — The EM Wave

Light is an oscillating electromagnetic field where the electric component E is perpendicular to the magnetic component B, and both are perpendicular to the propagation direction k. Most materials respond only to E, so the optical field refers to the E-field.

Monochromatic plane wave (1D)
$$E_x = E_o \cos(\omega t - kz + \phi_o)$$
Key relations
$$f=1/T,\quad \omega = 2\pi/T,\quad k = 2\pi/\lambda$$ $$v = \frac{dz}{dt}=\frac{\omega}{k}=\lambda f$$
📚 Light-related vocabulary (click to expand) ▼

Monochromatic: single or very narrow range of λ. Example: red laser ✅, the sun ❌.

Coherent: photons constituting the beam are in phase. Example: laser ✅, LED ❌.

Polarization: direction of the E-field. Sources can be unpolarized, plane, elliptical, or circularly polarized.

🌡️ Generation of light (incandescence / luminescence) ▼

Heated elements/molecules (▼): electrons excited to E₂ and relax to E₁, releasing photons.

The output spectrum depends solely on temperature $T$ — described by Planck's law of blackbody radiation:

$$S_\lambda = \frac{8\pi hc}{\lambda^5}\cdot\frac{1}{e^{hc/\lambda kT}-1}$$

Fun fact: The temperature of outer space is ~2.7 K (cosmic microwave background), peaking around λ ≈ 2 mm.

Light–Matter Interaction: R · A · T

Incident light is mostly Reflected, Absorbed, and Transmitted (and partly scattered).

$$I_o = I_R + I_A + I_T$$
Metals

Fine succession of energy states → near-surface electrons absorb visible light and re-emit → very reflective (R = 90–96 %). Opaque unless very thin (<100 nm).

Non-metals

Absorption is conditional on $h\nu > E_G$. Reflection depends on n: glass R ≈ 4 %, Si R ≈ 30 %.

Insulators (wide Eg)

$E_G > 3.1$ eV ⇒ no visible absorption ⇒ transparent (diamond, glass, sapphire).

🎨 Why do materials have color? (Eg rules) ▼
  • $E_G < 1.8$ eV → full absorption → black (Si @ 1.1, GaAs @ 1.4 eV).
  • $E_G > 3.1$ eV → no absorption → colorless/transparent (diamond @ 5.5, glass @ 9 eV).
  • $1.8 < E_G < 3.1$ eV or has impurity → partial absorption → colored.

Example: CdS has $E_G$ = 2.4 eV. It absorbs blue/violet and transmits red/yellow/orange, giving it a yellow-orange appearance.

Ruby: Al₂O₃ (Eg > 3.1 eV, colorless) + 0.5–2 at.% Cr₂O₃ → Cr³⁺ creates impurity levels absorbing blue and yellow → red transmitted ⇒ ruby red.

9.2  Refractive Index (n)

The refractive index measures how much light slows down in a material compared to vacuum. Atoms are polarized by E, creating a phase lag; larger permittivity → stronger drag → slower light.

$$n \equiv \frac{c}{v} = \sqrt{\varepsilon_r\,\mu_r} \approx \sqrt{\varepsilon_r}$$
(optical materials are non-magnetic, $\mu_r \approx 1$)
$$n = \frac{\lambda_o}{\lambda} = \frac{k}{k_o},\quad \lambda = \frac{\lambda_o}{n}$$
Frequency is preserved across interfaces; wavelength shrinks.

🧊 Optical Anisotropy — birefringence ▼

Glasses, liquids, and cubic crystals are optically isotropic (single n).

Non-cubic crystals are anisotropic/birefringent:

  • Uniaxial (tetragonal/hexagonal/trigonal): two indices $n_o$ (ordinary), $n_e$ (extraordinary).
  • Biaxial (orthorhombic/monoclinic/triclinic): three indices $n_1, n_2, n_3$.

Example: Calcite CaCO₃ has $n_o = 1.658$, $n_e = 1.486$ → the famous "double image".

Applications: polarising beam splitters, phase-contrast microscopy, LCDs, waveplates.

9.3–9.4  Dispersion n(λ) & Group Velocity

Dispersion occurs when plane waves of different wavelengths have different propagation velocities, causing a wave packet of mixed wavelengths to spread out in space (pulse broadening → higher BER).

Sellmeier equation
$$n^2(\lambda) = 1 + \sum_i \frac{A_i \lambda^2}{\lambda^2 - \lambda_i^2}$$
Cauchy equation
$$n(\lambda) = A + \frac{B}{\lambda^2} + \frac{C}{\lambda^4}$$
Group velocity & group index
$$v_g = \frac{\delta\omega}{\delta k} \approx \frac{c}{\,n - \lambda\,dn/d\lambda\,} = \frac{c}{N_g}$$
Zero-dispersion condition
$$\frac{dN_g}{d\lambda} = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \text{silica @ }\lambda \approx 1.3\,\mu\text{m}$$
📏 Example 9.6 — Silica @ 1300 nm ▼
n = 1.447 → v = c/n ≈ 2.07 × 10⁸ m/s
Nɢ = 1.462 → v_g = c/Nɢ ≈ 2.052 × 10⁸ m/s
At 1.3 µm, silica has zero material dispersion — ideal for long-haul fiber optics.

9.6  Snell's Law & Total Internal Reflection

For constructive interference, wavefronts of reflected and refracted waves must be in phase with the incident wave. This derivation (Snell, 1621) gives:

Snell's law
$$n_1 \sin\theta_i = n_2 \sin\theta_t$$
Critical angle (TIR, $n_1>n_2$)
$$\theta_c = \sin^{-1}\!\left(\tfrac{n_2}{n_1}\right)$$
Brewster's (polarization) angle
$$\theta_p = \tan^{-1}\!\left(\tfrac{n_2}{n_1}\right)$$
(a) θᵢ < θc → some transmitted (refracted), some reflected.
(c) θᵢ > θc → Total Internal Reflection. Evanescent wave decays into medium 2.
Evanescent wave decay (penetration depth = 1/α)
$$\alpha = \frac{2\pi n_2}{\lambda}\!\left[\!\left(\frac{n_1}{n_2}\right)^{\!2}\sin^2\theta_i - 1\right]^{1/2}$$

9.7  Fresnel's Equations (Magnitudes & Phases)

Fresnel (1818) derived four equations for reflection/transmission coefficients at a dielectric interface, for two polarizations (⊥ and ∥ to the plane of incidence), using Snell's law, boundary conditions, and Faraday's law ($\omega\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{E}$).

$$r_\perp = \frac{\cos\theta_i - \sqrt{n^2 - \sin^2\theta_i}}{\cos\theta_i + \sqrt{n^2 - \sin^2\theta_i}}$$
$$r_\parallel = \frac{\sqrt{n^2 - \sin^2\theta_i} - n^2\cos\theta_i}{\sqrt{n^2 - \sin^2\theta_i} + n^2\cos\theta_i}$$
Normal incidence (most practical)
$$R = \left(\frac{n_1 - n_2}{n_1 + n_2}\right)^{\!2},\quad T = \frac{4 n_1 n_2}{(n_1 + n_2)^2}$$
With absorption (complex N)
$$R = \frac{(n-1)^2 + K^2}{(n+1)^2 + K^2}$$
🔆 Design applications: ARC, Bragg mirrors, FBGs ▼

Anti-Reflection Coating (ARC) — solar cells & photodetectors. Insert a layer with $n_2 = \sqrt{n_1 n_3}$ and thickness $d = \lambda/(4n_2)$ so reflections cancel by destructive interference.

Quarter-wave Bragg reflector — DBRs in VCSELs and EUV lithography mirrors. Stack layers of alternating $n_1/n_2$ with quarter-wavelength thickness → constructive reflection, 100 % reflectance, no absorption (unlike metal mirrors).

Fiber Bragg Gratings (FBG) — periodic n modulation in fiber core (Ge-doped SiO₂, photosensitive to UV). Used as wavelength filters in DWDM systems.

9.8–9.11  Loss, Complex Index & Scattering

For metals and semiconductors, free electrons cause absorption, so we use a complex refractive index:

$$N = n - jK,\quad \varepsilon_r = \varepsilon_r' - j\varepsilon_r'' \;\;\Leftrightarrow\;\; n, K$$ $$n^2 - K^2 = \varepsilon_r',\quad 2nK = \varepsilon_r''$$
Beer–Lambert law (intensity attenuation)
$$I(z) = I_o\,e^{-\alpha z}, \quad \alpha = 2k'' = 2 k_o K$$
Band-to-band cut-off
$$E_G = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \;\Rightarrow\; \lambda_{\text{cut-off}}(\mu\text{m}) = \frac{1.24}{E_G(\text{eV})}$$

9.12  Optical Fibers & EDFA

Silica fibers suffer intrinsic loss from lattice absorption (IR tail, Si–O at 9 µm) and Rayleigh scattering ($\propto 1/\lambda^4$). The optimum is ≈ 1.55 µm with just 0.16 dB/km — the theoretical limit.

⚡ EDFA — Erbium-Doped Fiber Amplifier ▼

A ~30 m section of SiO₂ fiber doped with Er³⁺ ions ([Xe] 4f¹¹). A 980 nm laser diode pumps Er³⁺ from ⁴I₁₅/₂ to ⁴I₁₁/₂, which quickly relaxes non-radiatively to the metastable ⁴I₁₃/₂ state.

An incoming weak signal at ~1480 nm triggers stimulated emission: 1 photon in → 2 photons out. This amplifies the signal in the fiber without electrical conversion — a huge enabler for long-haul optical networks.

9.13  Luminescence & Quantum Dots

Luminescence: emission of radiation due to absorption and conversion of non-thermal energy. The emission is always lower in energy than the excitation (Stokes shift).

Fluorescence
Emission < 10 ns after excitation
Phosphorescence
Emission > 10 ns (can be hours, e.g. Hope Diamond)
Photoluminescence (PL)
Excited by photons (banknote fibers)
Cathodoluminescence (CL)
Excited by energetic electrons (CRT)

Quantum Dots: size-dependent color

Confined electrons in a 3D box have discrete levels:

$$E(n_x,n_y,n_z) = \frac{h^2}{8m^*}\!\left(\frac{n_x^2}{a^2} + \frac{n_y^2}{b^2} + \frac{n_z^2}{c^2}\right)$$

Smaller dots → larger Eg → bluer emission. Example: CdSe QDs of 2–6 nm span blue → green → yellow → orange → red.

White LEDs

A blue InGaN chip (≈ 450 nm) pumps a yellow YAG:Ce³⁺ phosphor. The combined spectrum (blue + yellow complementary colors) appears white to the eye.

Part II  Magnetic Materials (companion overview)

Note: The uploaded deck focuses on Optical Properties. This brief section is provided for companion reference.

Key quantities

$$\mathbf{B} = \mu_0(\mathbf{H} + \mathbf{M}),\quad \mathbf{M} = \chi_m \mathbf{H}$$
$$\mu_r = 1 + \chi_m, \quad \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mu_r \mathbf{H}$$

Types of magnetism

TypeχₘOriginExamples
Diamagneticsmall, negative (~−10⁻⁵)Induced orbital currents oppose HCu, Au, H₂O, Bi
Paramagneticsmall, positive (~10⁻³)Unpaired spins, thermal disorderAl, Pt, O₂
Ferromagneticlarge (~10³–10⁵)Exchange-aligned domains below TcFe, Co, Ni
AntiferromagneticsmallAdjacent spins antiparallelMnO, Cr
FerrimagneticlargeAntiparallel spins with unequal magnitudeFe₃O₄, ferrites

Important concepts

  • Magnetic domains: regions of aligned moments within a ferromagnet; separated by domain walls (Bloch walls).
  • Hysteresis loop (B–H): coercivity Hc, remanence Br, saturation Bs. Soft magnets (low Hc) vs. hard magnets (high Hc).
  • Curie temperature Tc: above Tc, thermal energy randomizes spins → paramagnetic.
  • Superconductivity: perfect diamagnetism below Tc (Meissner effect); expels magnetic flux from interior.

🗒️ Cheat Sheet — All Formulas & Key Facts

Wave & Propagation

$$E_x = E_o \cos(\omega t - kz + \phi_o)$$
$$v = \omega/k = \lambda f,\; k = 2\pi/\lambda,\; \omega = 2\pi f$$

Refractive Index & Dispersion

$$n = c/v = \sqrt{\varepsilon_r}$$
$$v_g = c/N_g, \quad N_g = n - \lambda\,dn/d\lambda$$
Sellmeier: $n^2 = 1 + \sum \frac{A_i \lambda^2}{\lambda^2-\lambda_i^2}$
Cauchy: $n = A + B/\lambda^2 + C/\lambda^4$

Interfaces (Snell / Fresnel)

$$n_1 \sin\theta_i = n_2 \sin\theta_t$$
$$\theta_c = \sin^{-1}(n_2/n_1),\; \theta_p = \tan^{-1}(n_2/n_1)$$
Normal incidence: $R = \big(\tfrac{n_1-n_2}{n_1+n_2}\big)^2$
$T = \tfrac{4n_1n_2}{(n_1+n_2)^2}, \; R+T = 1$
With K: $R = \tfrac{(n-1)^2 + K^2}{(n+1)^2 + K^2}$
ARC: $n_2 = \sqrt{n_1 n_3},\; d = \lambda/(4n_2)$

Loss & Absorption

$$N = n - jK$$
$$\alpha = 2k'' = 2k_o K$$
$$I(z) = I_o e^{-\alpha z}\quad\text{(Beer's law)}$$
$$\lambda_\text{cut-off}(\mu m) = 1.24/E_G(eV)$$
Rayleigh: $I \propto 1/\lambda^4$
Evanescent decay: $\alpha_\text{ev} = \tfrac{2\pi n_2}{\lambda}\sqrt{(n_1/n_2)^2 \sin^2\theta_i - 1}$

Quantum & Luminescence

$$E_n = \frac{h^2 n^2}{8 m^* a^2}\text{ (1D box)}$$
$$E(n_x,n_y,n_z) = \tfrac{h^2}{8m^*}\!\left(\tfrac{n_x^2}{a^2}+\tfrac{n_y^2}{b^2}+\tfrac{n_z^2}{c^2}\right)$$

📌 Must-remember numbers

  • Visible light: 400–700 nm, 1.8–3.1 eV
  • Fiber comm windows: 1.31 µm (zero dispersion) & 1.55 µm (min loss ≈ 0.16 dB/km)
  • Silica fiber at 1300 nm: n = 1.447, Nɢ = 1.462
  • R for air/glass (n=1.5): ~4 % ; for air/Si (n=3.5): ~31 %
  • Metals: R ≈ 90–96 %; ARC: make $n_2 = \sqrt{n_1 n_3}$
  • Brewster's for glass: θp ≈ 56° (from air)
  • EDFA pump: 980 nm → amplifies signal at ~1480–1550 nm
  • EUV lithography: Sn plasma @ 13.5 nm (92 eV)
